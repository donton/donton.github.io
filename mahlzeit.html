Introduction

Mahlzeit is a software company that develops workplace information systems to improve company canteens and cafeterias. Their products are designed to improve the lunch-at-work experience by offering the end-user more insight into the daily operations of the workplace restaurant. During my time there, their product consisted of a crowd flow monitoring system and an end-user interface accessible through their apps. Users would find updated information about their workplace restaurant such as the day-of menu and live busyness on the apps.

I worked at Mahlzeit during its beginning stages as a UX Design Intern and worked to improve the interface of their app, website and TV dashboard. This page highlights my journey to develop the company's TV dashboard app.

Discovery & Research

My discovery and research stage primarily consisted of determining the company's needs in the dashboard as well as surveying potential users about what they wanted in their workplace.

Goals

  • Display the day-of restaurant menu
  • Include a social feed
  • Have live information about the crowd flow and busyness in the restaurant
  • Accomodate featured company information such as specials and events

With user preferences and the company's software capabilities in mind, I created a user persona to represent the typical person who would use the TV dashboard.

Goals

  • Be able to plan her time better

Frustrations

Anna is an enthusiastic Creative Director at PWC with a lot on her plate. She works five days a week in a co-working space with other large companies. Her building has a cafeteria/restaurant, but with all the employees from the different companies, it is often busy and hectic during peak lunch hours. When she does find time to get lunch, she often comes in blind not knowing what's being served and is sometimes unsatisfied with the offering. Conceptual Design

With the companies goals and user needs in mind, I drafted up some designs for the TV dashboard. The essentials for the design included the busyness, menu and social components.

Three Main Components

Busyness

Live crowd flow information with current capacity percentage

Historical traffic index that shows popular times for the restaurant

Recommendation on when to go to the restaurant

Menu

Restuarant offerings for the day

Description and allergen information

Can be comprised of images, text or both

Social

Shows company's social media feeds from different platforms (Facebook, Instagram, Twitter)

Ability to show company announcements and other posts

Client Testing

Feedback

Further Testing

With these changes in mind, I created updated iterations of the design. Further testing was done in-house with users in our building as well as designers to nail down the graphics. We set up our own screens to test the legibility of the components.

Final Design

After creating multiple iterations of the dashboard, testing with different companies and fine-tuning details, I developed the final prototype for our company's building. The sped up version is provided to show complete range of the dashboard.

This is the final version of the TV Dashboard for our building, B. Business. The design adheres to B.'s style guidelines while maintaining our general layout. While the menu section contains the day's food choices, it also displays the smoothies of the week and upcoming events. The busyness sections allows the user to quickly glance at the screen and gather all the information they need to decide when to eat. By using a "conveyer belt" system, I was able to fit more information on the screen without it being too cluttered. Towards the end of my internship, I worked closely with our developer to help build the system.

Final Thoughts

During my internship at Mahlzeit, I was able to follow the UX Design process closely to develop a unique product â€“ a TV dashboard app. This experience gave me the opportunity to think critically about how to make a product usable without a user actually not being able to "use" it. This wasn't a typical smartphone app or website design, but it was a medium that people can't touch or interact with â€“ it had to be usable in the sense that a person can look at it and completely understand what's going on. This was a challenge I was proud of.

This internship also allowed me to have more freedom and control. Because the company was in its beginning stages, the team was small. I was the only UX designer, so I had a lot on my plate, but I was ready to manage. I spent a lot of my time working independently when I wasn't working with the team. I worked directly with the CEO and developers to help deliver a product to clients that they would pay for. I was also given the opportunity to work with people from other companies in our co-working space to get feedback and help when needed.

This experience has made me a better communicator, designer and user. I created a product that I, along with the company, are proud of. Designing the face of the company came with many pressures. I had to make sure that clients are instantly hooked with the visual impact. One thing I will take away from this experience is to better document user testing and interviews. I was writing and typing most things down, but recording might be better. Overall, it was a great opportunity for me to grow as a UX designer.
